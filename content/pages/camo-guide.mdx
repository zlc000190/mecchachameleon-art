Good camouflage is a combination of surface choice, paint, lighting, and pose. A nearly correct colour cannot rescue a silhouette that looks unmistakably human, and a clever pose cannot rescue bright paint on a dark wall.

Use this process before experimenting with advanced hiding tricks.

## 1. Choose the surface first

Do not build a colour and then search for somewhere to use it. Pick a wall, floor, curtain, shelf, or prop group first. Look for surfaces that are large enough to cover your body and detailed enough to hide small mistakes.

Beginner-friendly surfaces have two or three easy colours and stable lighting. Reflective floors, gradients, and moving lights are harder because the correct colour changes with the camera angle.

## 2. Identify primary and secondary colours

The primary colour covers most of the surface. The secondary colour explains the pattern, edge, mortar, shadow, or trim. Matching both prevents your body from appearing as one flat block.

Squint at the surface or view it from farther away. The colour that remains most visible is usually the primary one. Then inspect the edges and darkest repeated details for the secondary colour.

## 3. Account for light and shadow

Paint values do not exist independently of the room. The same wall can look warm near a lamp and cool near a window. Stand inside the same lighting zone as your target surface.

Check your cast shadow. If the nearby shelf casts left but your body creates a strong shadow in another direction, the Seeker can notice the inconsistency even when your paint is accurate.

## 4. Break up the silhouette

Turn the body so its longest line follows a wall edge, shelf, curtain fold, or stack of objects. Keep arms and legs from creating isolated shapes.

A compact pose is reliable for beginners. Wider or stranger poses can merge with furniture, but they are easier to get wrong and may only work from one angle.

## 5. Test from the Seeker's angle

Do not judge the camouflage only from your own close camera. Imagine the first doorway, corridor, or spawn route the Seeker will use. Step back if the game allows it and check the spot from more than one side.

The strongest setup survives a quick distant scan. It does not need to look perfect at arm's length if the route never brings the Seeker that close.

## Common camouflage mistakes

- Matching only one colour on a multi-colour surface.
- Choosing a famous dark corner with no visual clutter.
- Crossing a straight edge with part of the body.
- Ignoring the colour temperature of local lighting.
- Moving after the Seeker enters the room.
- Using a complex pose that only works from one direction.

## A 20-second camo routine

1. Pick the surface.
2. Find the main colour.
3. Find the repeated secondary colour or shadow.
4. Turn your narrowest side toward the Seeker route.
5. Align the pose with nearby shapes.
6. Stop moving and verify the outline once.

Practise this routine on easy surfaces before using high-risk spots from the [hiding spots guide](/hiding-spots).

## Frequently asked questions

### Is exact colour matching more important than the pose?

They work together. Colour wins the first distant glance; pose and shadow decide whether the Seeker stops for a closer look.

### Why does my paint look right in one place and wrong in another?

Lighting changes the visible colour. Match the surface in the same light zone where you plan to hide.

### Are patterned surfaces too difficult?

Simple repeating patterns are often helpful because they break the outline. Use the primary colour for most of the body and the secondary colour where the pattern repeats.
