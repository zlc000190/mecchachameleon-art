The best Meccha Chameleon hiding spots are not simply the darkest corners. Strong spots combine a believable surface, matching paint, a compact silhouette, visual clutter, and a Seeker route that does not force a close inspection.

Use this page as the strategy layer, then open the [map atlas](/maps) for screenshots, paint colours, difficulty labels, and individual locations.

## What makes a hiding spot strong?

A strong spot gives the Seeker more than one reason to ignore you:

- Your primary and secondary paint colours match the surface.
- Your body outline overlaps furniture, trim, posters, books, or other shapes.
- The local lighting creates a believable shadow.
- The spot is outside the Seeker's first obvious sweep.
- You can reach it before the preparation timer becomes a problem.

The safest beginner spot is usually simple and repeatable, not spectacular. A difficult spot is only valuable when you can paint and pose correctly under time pressure.

## Five useful hiding-spot types

### Patterned walls

Wallpaper, brick, posters, and tile break up your outline. Copy both the background and the smaller pattern colour. Avoid standing across a clean border where your body interrupts a straight line.

### Shelves and stacked props

Books, crates, cushions, and boxes create many overlapping silhouettes. Turn your narrowest side toward the expected Seeker route and align your limbs with the stack.

### Floor transitions

The border between a rug and floor can be effective when your paint includes both colours. It is risky when the Seeker approaches from above, so test the camera angle before committing.

### Door frames and architectural trim

Frames provide strong vertical lines that can hide part of the body. They are also common travel routes, so use them only when your pose does not block movement or cast an unnatural shadow.

### Visually noisy corners

A corner filled with plants, signs, cables, or furniture is often stronger than an empty dark corner. The goal is not darkness; it is making the Seeker process too many shapes at once.

## Read the Seeker route

Think about where the Seeker appears and which direction most players look first. A good surface can become a weak spot when it sits directly in the opening sightline.

Choose a position that survives two checks: the first fast scan and the later return scan. If a spot is famous, assume experienced Seekers will inspect it even when your paint is good.

## Beginner, medium, and hard spots

- **Beginner:** quick to reach, one clear surface, simple pose, low colour complexity.
- **Medium:** needs two colours, a specific angle, or a short movement after the round starts.
- **Hard:** depends on exact lighting, a narrow sightline, advanced pose control, or detailed map knowledge.

Do not confuse difficulty with quality. A beginner spot that works consistently is better than a difficult spot you miss half the time.

## Hiding-spot checklist

Before the Seeker enters, ask:

1. Does my outline look like a player from the main route?
2. Are my brightest and darkest colours both believable here?
3. Does my shadow match the nearby objects?
4. Am I crossing a clean edge or repeating pattern?
5. Can I stay completely still?

If one answer is clearly wrong, change the pose or move to a simpler surface.

## Frequently asked questions

### Where should a new player hide first?

Choose a patterned wall or a group of medium-sized props that is easy to reach. Avoid famous trick spots until you can paint quickly.

### Are map-edge spots always best?

No. Seekers know that beginners run to the edge. A central but visually noisy area can be safer because it looks too exposed to be a hiding place.

### Should I reuse the same spot?

Reuse it while learning paint and pose, but rotate once other players recognise the route. Private lobbies develop habits quickly.
